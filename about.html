Who is she

Yayoi Kusama, (born March 22, 1929, Matsumoto, Japan), Japanese artist who was a self-described “obsessional artist,” known for her extensive use of polka dots and for her infinity installations. She employed painting, sculpture, performance art, and installations in a variety of styles, including Pop art and Minimalism.

Yayoi Kusama's artistic path was shaped by a haunting childhood hallucination—a field of talking flowers transformed into endless dots that swallowed her existence. This strange encounter had an indelible impact on her later work. Kusama's all-over marks and dots aim to dissolve boundaries, fusing herself and her creations with the vast universe. Polka dots, to her, represent a path to infinity, a unity with our surroundings. By obliterating nature and bodies with dots, she invites us to consider our place as a single polka dot among countless stars, merging into the interconnected fabric of existence. Experience the profound connection and limitless possibilities of Kusama's dot-filled world.

Yayoi Kusama defied her parents' wishes to pursue her artistic career. Even when her drawings were ripped up, she persisted, making do with mud and old sacks when art supplies were scarce. She persuaded her parents to let her study painting at art school, determined to make her mark. She moved to New York in the late 1950s and immersed herself in the city's vibrant art scene. Her "infinity net" paintings, distinguished by repetitive marks that extended beyond the canvas, pushed the boundaries of painting and anticipated Minimalism. Meetings with influential artists such as Donald Judd and Andy Warhol fueled Kusama's artistic journey even further. She became a multidisciplinary creative force, experimenting with music, design, writing, and fashion.
